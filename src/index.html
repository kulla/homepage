Some of my projects

Serlo Editor

Lead Software Developer

2022 - present

Together with others, I work to enable teachers to create didactically high‑quality learning materials as easily, quickly, and reliably as possible. To this end, we are developing the Serlo Editor — a web‑based editor for digital and interactive learning materials.

The central technical challenge of the project is to combine an intuitive WYSIWYG user experience, comparable to tools like Word and Google Docs, with the ability to create highly structured content such as interactive exercises. To the best of our current knowledge, the Serlo Editor is one of the few projects that successfully bridges these two worlds.

serlo.org - The Open Learning Platform

Lead Software Developer

2020 - 2025

Serlo.org is a non-profit, open learning platform that provides free, openly licensed educational resources for students and teachers. Its mission is to enable equitable access to high-quality education by making learning materials freely available and collaboratively developed by a community of educators, subject-matter experts, and volunteers.

At serlo.org, I led a development team of up to twelve engineers. During this time, we rebuilt the entire platform using a microservice architecture, with a Next.js/React frontend and an Apollo Server (GraphQL) / Node.js backend. Due to the foundation’s relatively small size, I was also involved beyond software development, contributing to areas such as fundraising and financial planning.

Open textbook "Mathe für Nicht-Freaks"

Founder & Author

2009 - present

I am driven by the conviction that every university student should be able to understand mathematics. Guided by this vision, I co-founded the open textbook “Mathe für Nicht-Freaks” (“Maths for Non-Freaks”) together with a growing community of authors.

We reach this vision by teaching the intuition and motivation behind abstract mathematical concepts and by disrupting the definition-theorem-proof scheme of mathematical education in universities. We estimate that the open textbook reaches approximately 30% of all first-semester university students in STEM fields in Germany.
